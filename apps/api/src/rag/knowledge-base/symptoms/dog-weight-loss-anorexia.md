## Pattern Overview

Progressive weight loss combined with decreased appetite (anorexia or hyporexia) in a dog indicates significant systemic disease in most cases. This presentation warrants a thorough diagnostic workup because the underlying causes span chronic organ disease, endocrine disorders, infectious disease, neoplasia, and gastrointestinal conditions. A complete minimum database (CBC + chemistry + urinalysis) is almost always the correct first step.

This document supports AI-assisted triage and test recommendation. It does not constitute a diagnosis.

## Common Symptoms

- Progressive weight loss over weeks to months
- Decreased or absent appetite
- Muscle wasting (cachexia)
- Lethargy, reduced activity
- Vomiting or diarrhea (if GI cause)
- Increased or decreased thirst and urination
- Abdominal enlargement (organomegaly, ascites)
- Poor coat condition

## Possible Associated Diseases

| Disease / Pattern                                  | Key Differentiating Feature                                                              |
| -------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Chronic kidney disease (CKD)                       | PU/PD; elevated CREA + BUN; low urine SG; older dog                                      |
| Chronic liver disease                              | Elevated liver enzymes; low ALB + BUN; possible icterus                                  |
| Neoplasia (lymphoma, GI tumor, etc.)               | Palpable mass or lymphadenopathy; imaging often needed                                   |
| Hypoadrenocorticism (Addison's disease)            | Waxing-waning GI signs; low Na, high K; young–middle age dog                             |
| Diabetes mellitus                                  | PU/PD; high glucose; glucosuria; cataracts                                               |
| Protein-losing enteropathy (IBD, lymphangiectasia) | Low ALB + TP; normal liver enzymes; chronic diarrhea                                     |
| Chronic infectious disease (Ehrlichia, Leishmania) | Lymphadenopathy; hyperglobulinemia; endemic area                                         |
| Dental disease / oral pain                         | Reluctance to chew; halitosis; oral lesions visible on exam                              |
| Cardiac cachexia                                   | Concurrent heart disease; ascites; cough                                                 |
| Exocrine pancreatic insufficiency (EPI)            | Voluminous pale stool; polyphagia despite weight loss; young German Shepherd predisposed |

## Recommended Diagnostic Tests

**First-line — minimum database:**

- **CBC with differential** — anemia pattern, inflammatory response, lymphocyte count
- **Comprehensive serum chemistry panel** — liver, kidney, glucose, protein, electrolytes
- **Urinalysis with USG** — renal concentrating ability, glucosuria, proteinuria

**Second-line (guided by first-line findings):**

- **Urine protein:creatinine ratio (UPC)** — if proteinuria on urinalysis
- **Abdominal ultrasound** — hepatomegaly, splenomegaly, masses, lymphadenopathy, intestinal wall changes
- **Thoracic radiographs** — pulmonary masses, cardiac disease, metastatic disease
- **Serum cortisol / ACTH stimulation test** — if Addison's disease suspected (low Na, high K, history of GI crises)
- **Serum T4 + TSH** — hypothyroidism (less common cause of weight loss; more often weight gain — but relevant in differential)
- **Tick-borne disease panel** — if hyperglobulinemia + thrombocytopenia or endemic area
- **Trypsin-like immunoreactivity (TLI)** — if EPI suspected (young dog, large breed, polyphagia)
- **Serum B12/folate** — if chronic GI signs + weight loss (small intestinal disease)

## When to Prioritize Urgent Evaluation

- Rapid weight loss (> 10% body weight in 1–2 weeks) — likely active disease
- Complete anorexia lasting > 3–5 days
- Severe hypoalbuminemia (ALB < 1.5 g/dL) — risk of edema, effusions
- Suspected Addison's disease crisis: weakness + vomiting + bradycardia + low Na + high K
- Abdominal mass detected on palpation — neoplasia until proven otherwise
- Jaundice + weight loss — liver disease or hemolytic anemia
