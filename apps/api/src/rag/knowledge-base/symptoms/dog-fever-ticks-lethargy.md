## Pattern Overview

Fever combined with tick exposure history and lethargy is one of the most important clinical presentations in dogs in tick-endemic regions (including Colombia and Latin America). This triad strongly suggests a tick-borne disease until proven otherwise. The most common agents are _Ehrlichia canis_, _Anaplasma_ spp., and _Babesia_ spp. — which can also occur as co-infections. Early diagnosis and treatment significantly improve outcomes.

This document supports AI-assisted triage and test recommendation. It does not constitute a diagnosis.

## Common Symptoms

- Fever (often > 39.5°C / 103°F)
- Lethargy, depression, reluctance to move
- Anorexia or reduced appetite
- Petechiae, ecchymoses, or epistaxis (bleeding signs)
- Pale or slightly icteric mucous membranes
- Lymphadenopathy or splenomegaly on palpation
- Joint pain, stiffness, lameness (Anaplasma)
- Recent tick exposure or tick attachment history

## Possible Associated Diseases

| Disease                                    | Key Differentiating Feature                                 |
| ------------------------------------------ | ----------------------------------------------------------- |
| Ehrlichiosis (_Ehrlichia canis_)           | Severe thrombocytopenia; hyperglobulinemia in chronic phase |
| Anaplasmosis (_Anaplasma phagocytophilum_) | Neutropenia; joint pain; morulae in neutrophils on smear    |
| Babesiosis (_Babesia canis/vogeli_)        | Hemolytic anemia; icterus; hemoglobinuria                   |
| Leptospirosis                              | Renal involvement; jaundice; exposure to water/wildlife     |
| Rickettsial disease (_R. rickettsii_)      | Petechial rash; acute severe illness                        |
| Non-specific fever of infection            | Diagnosis of exclusion after tick-borne agents ruled out    |

## Recommended Diagnostic Tests

**First-line (order immediately):**

- **CBC with differential** — thrombocytopenia is the most consistent finding; assess severity
- **Serum chemistry panel (ALT, AST, BUN, CREA, ALB, TP, GLOB)** — organ involvement
- **Blood smear** — look for morulae (Anaplasma in neutrophils; Ehrlichia in monocytes) and Babesia piroplasms in RBCs

**Confirmatory (based on CBC/smear results):**

- **Tick-borne disease panel / vector-borne panel** — serology (IFA/ELISA) or PCR for Ehrlichia, Anaplasma, Babesia
- **Babesia PCR** — if hemolytic anemia or icterus present
- **Leptospira serology (MAT)** — if azotemia or jaundice without tick-borne pattern
- **Urinalysis** — assess renal involvement, hemoglobinuria

## When to Prioritize Urgent Evaluation

Escalate urgency if any of the following are present:

- Spontaneous bleeding (epistaxis, melena, petechiae that is spreading)
- Severe pallor or icterus — suggests acute hemolysis (Babesia emergency)
- Hemoglobinuria (dark red/brown urine) — intravascular hemolysis
- Collapse, weakness, or inability to stand
- PLT < 20,000/μL or HCT < 20% on CBC
- Signs of septic shock (tachycardia, weak pulse, hypothermia)
