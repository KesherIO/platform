## Pattern Overview

Cough combined with nasal and/or ocular discharge in dogs suggests respiratory tract disease, which may be infectious, inflammatory, or structural. In unvaccinated dogs — especially puppies — Canine Distemper Virus (CDV) must be considered as a serious possibility. In vaccinated adult dogs, infectious tracheobronchitis (kennel cough) is far more common. The progression pattern, vaccination history, and presence of neurological signs are critical for triage.

This document supports AI-assisted triage and test recommendation. It does not constitute a diagnosis.

## Common Symptoms

- Dry or productive cough (may be harsh, honking, or wet)
- Nasal discharge (serous → mucopurulent as disease progresses)
- Ocular discharge (serous → mucopurulent)
- Sneezing, reverse sneezing
- Fever, lethargy, anorexia (systemic involvement)
- Dyspnea or labored breathing (pneumonia)
- Hyperkeratosis of nose/footpads (Distemper — late sign)
- Myoclonus or neurological signs (Distemper — serious sign)

## Possible Associated Diseases

| Disease / Pattern                                            | Key Differentiating Feature                                                        |
| ------------------------------------------------------------ | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Canine Distemper Virus (CDV)                                 | Unvaccinated; lymphopenia; multisystemic (respiratory + GI + neurological)         |
| Infectious tracheobronchitis (kennel cough, Bordetella, CPI) | Vaccinated adult; harsh cough; recent kennel/shelter exposure; mild systemic signs |
| Canine influenza (CIV H3N8 / H3N2)                           | Sudden onset; high morbidity in group; mucopurulent discharge                      |
| Bacterial pneumonia (secondary)                              | Fever; productive cough; radiographic consolidation; neutrophilia                  |
| Aspiration pneumonia                                         | History of megaesophagus, vomiting, or anesthesia; ventral lung lobe distribution  |
| Fungal infection (_Aspergillus_, _Cryptococcus_)             | Chronic course; nasal lesions; skull/sinus involvement                             |
| Nasal foreign body                                           | Unilateral nasal discharge; sneezing; acute onset                                  |
| Cardiac disease with pulmonary edema                         | Older dog; heart murmur; nocturnal cough; exercise intolerance                     |

## Recommended Diagnostic Tests

**First-line:**

- **CBC with differential** — lymphopenia strongly suggests CDV; neutrophilia + left shift suggests bacterial pneumonia
- **Thoracic radiographs (2 views)** — assess lung pattern, heart size, bronchial/interstitial/alveolar changes

**If CDV suspected (unvaccinated, lymphopenia, multisystemic signs):**

- **CDV antigen rapid test (conjunctival swab or urine)** — in-clinic first screen
- **CDV PCR (blood, conjunctival swab, CSF, urine)** — confirmatory; most sensitive in acute phase
- **Blood smear** — look for distemper inclusion bodies in RBCs and leukocytes (low sensitivity but pathognomonic when positive)

**If bacterial pneumonia suspected:**

- **Tracheal wash or bronchoalveolar lavage (BAL) + cytology + culture** — identifies pathogen; guides antibiotic choice
- **Blood culture** — if sepsis suspected (high fever, clinical deterioration)

**If cardiac disease considered:**

- **Cardiac biomarkers (NT-proBNP)** — elevated in cardiac disease
- **Echocardiography** — structural cardiac evaluation

## When to Prioritize Urgent Evaluation

- Respiratory distress: open-mouth breathing, cyanosis, orthopnea (sits up to breathe) → **emergency**
- Neurological signs alongside respiratory signs → CDV or other systemic disease → high urgency
- Unvaccinated puppy with lymphopenia + fever + mucopurulent discharge → CDV protocol
- High fever (> 40°C / 104°F) + productive cough + dyspnea → bacterial pneumonia
- Hemoptysis (blood in secretions or coughed up)
- Rapid progression from dry cough to dyspnea within 24–48 hours
