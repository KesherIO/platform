## Pattern Overview

Vomiting and diarrhea in puppies (< 6 months) and kittens (< 6 months) is a medical emergency until proven otherwise. Young animals have immature immune systems, minimal nutritional reserves, and decompensate rapidly. The leading life-threatening causes are **Canine Parvovirus (CPV-2)** in puppies and **Feline Panleukopenia Virus (FPV)** in kittens — both cause severe leukopenia and carry high mortality without aggressive treatment. Hypoglycemia is a concurrent life-threatening complication that must be checked immediately.

This document supports AI-assisted triage and test recommendation. It does not constitute a diagnosis.

## Common Symptoms

- Vomiting (acute, often severe)
- Diarrhea (watery, mucoid, or hemorrhagic — characteristic foul odor in Parvovirus)
- Severe lethargy, depression, weakness
- Fever (may be hypothermic in shock)
- Dehydration (sunken eyes, dry mucous membranes, reduced skin elasticity)
- Anorexia
- Abdominal pain
- Signs of shock in advanced cases: pale gums, tachycardia, weak pulse, cold extremities

## Possible Associated Diseases

| Disease / Pattern                                               | Key Differentiating Feature                                       |
| --------------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Canine Parvovirus CPV-2 (puppy)                                 | Unvaccinated; bloody diarrhea; severe leukopenia/neutropenia      |
| Feline Panleukopenia FPV (kitten)                               | Unvaccinated; bloody diarrhea; panleukopenia; similar to CPV      |
| Intestinal parasites (Giardia, coccidia, hookworms, roundworms) | Common in young animals; fecal flotation diagnostic               |
| Canine Distemper (puppy)                                        | Multisystemic (respiratory + GI); lymphopenia; unvaccinated       |
| Dietary change or dietary indiscretion                          | Mild signs; self-limiting; normal CBC                             |
| Bacterial gastroenteritis                                       | Moderate neutrophilia; positive fecal culture                     |
| Intussusception                                                 | Persistent vomiting; palpable sausage-shaped mass; imaging needed |
| Coronavirus (canine, feline)                                    | Mild to moderate; self-limiting; uncommon severe form             |

## Recommended Diagnostic Tests

**Immediate (perform before any other workup):**

- **Blood glucose (point-of-care glucometer)** — hypoglycemia (< 60 mg/dL) is rapidly fatal in young animals and must be corrected immediately
- **Fecal Parvovirus antigen test (SNAP/ELISA)** — **for all unvaccinated or incompletely vaccinated puppies with GI signs** — high sensitivity and specificity
- **Fecal Panleukopenia antigen test or fecal Parvovirus test** — **for all unvaccinated or incompletely vaccinated kittens** — CPV tests cross-react with FPV

**First-line:**

- **CBC with differential** — WBC < 2,000/μL with leukopenia is virtually diagnostic for Parvovirus/Panleukopenia in unvaccinated young animals
- **Fecal parasite exam (flotation + direct smear)** — Giardia, coccidia, hookworms, roundworms — concurrent infection is common

**Second-line:**

- **Serum chemistry panel** — BUN, CREA, ALB, TP — guides fluid and nutritional support
- **Abdominal ultrasound** — if obstruction or intussusception suspected
- **Serial blood glucose monitoring (q4–6h)** — continue throughout hospitalization in young animals

## When to Prioritize Urgent Evaluation

These signs in a puppy or kitten with vomiting/diarrhea require **immediate urgent evaluation:**

- Blood glucose < 60 mg/dL → **treat hypoglycemia immediately**
- WBC < 2,000/μL → severe immunosuppression; high sepsis risk
- Positive Parvovirus/Panleukopenia antigen test → hospitalization for intensive supportive care
- Hemorrhagic diarrhea (pure blood) in a puppy or kitten
- Signs of shock (pale gums, tachycardia > 180 bpm, weak pulse, hypothermia)
- Dehydration > 8% estimated — IV fluid resuscitation needed
- Persistent vomiting without diarrhea — rule out obstruction with imaging
