## Pattern Overview

Fever combined with oral lesions — including ulcers, stomatitis, gingivitis, or vesicles — is an important presentation in cats with a focused differential. The most clinically significant causes are FIV-associated chronic stomatitis, Feline Calicivirus (FCV), and idiopathic/immune-mediated gingivostomatitis. In cats, oral pain causes significant anorexia and rapid secondary deterioration (hepatic lipidosis risk). Rapid identification and management of the underlying cause is important.

This document supports AI-assisted triage and test recommendation. It does not constitute a diagnosis.

## Common Symptoms

- Oral ulcers, erosions, or vesicles (tongue, palate, lips, gingiva)
- Stomatitis or gingivitis (red, inflamed gingiva)
- Fever (temperature > 39.5°C / 103°F)
- Hypersalivation / ptyalism
- Anorexia (oral pain)
- Halitosis (severe bad breath)
- Nasal discharge, sneezing (calicivirus — concurrent URI)
- Limping (calicivirus — limping syndrome in some strains)
- Weight loss from chronic pain and reduced eating

## Possible Associated Diseases

| Disease / Pattern            | Key Differentiating Feature                                                                     |
| ---------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| FIV-associated stomatitis    | Adult outdoor cat; refractory gingivitis/stomatitis; FIV antibody positive; lymphopenia         |
| Feline Calicivirus (FCV)     | Tongue/palate vesicles and ulcers; concurrent URI (sneezing, nasal discharge); often young cats |
| Feline Herpesvirus (FHV-1)   | Keratoconjunctivitis; ocular discharge predominates; nasal ulcers                               |
| Idiopathic gingivostomatitis | FIV/FeLV-negative; severe refractory stomatitis; immune dysregulation                           |
| Uremic stomatitis            | Oral ulcers + uremic breath in a cat with advanced CKD; elevated BUN + CREA                     |
| Feline Panleukopenia         | Oral ulcers less typical; severe leukopenia; GI signs predominate                               |
| Autoimmune (pemphigus)       | Skin + oral lesions; biopsy diagnostic                                                          |

## Recommended Diagnostic Tests

**First-line:**

- **FIV antibody + FeLV antigen test** — mandatory in all cats with stomatitis; FIV is a leading cause of refractory stomatitis
- **CBC with differential** — lymphopenia (FIV); leukopenia (viral); neutrophilia (bacterial secondary)
- **Oral examination under sedation** — proper characterization of lesion distribution and severity

**If calicivirus suspected:**

- **FCV PCR (oropharyngeal swab)** — confirms active shedding; differentiates from herpesvirus
- **FHV-1 PCR (conjunctival/nasal swab)** — concurrent herpesvirus infection common

**If CKD-related stomatitis suspected:**

- **Serum chemistry panel (BUN, CREA, SDMA, PHOS)** — assess renal function
- **Urinalysis with USG** — concentrating ability

**If idiopathic stomatitis:**

- **Dental radiographs** — root resorption lesions often co-present
- **Gingival biopsy** — histopathology; rules out lymphoma and identifies inflammatory pattern

## When to Prioritize Urgent Evaluation

- Inability to eat or drink for > 48 hours due to oral pain — dehydration + hepatic lipidosis risk
- Fever > 40°C / 104°F with oral lesions — systemic infection or severe caliciviral disease
- Respiratory involvement alongside oral lesions (calicivirus can cause acute severe pneumonia in virulent strains — FISS)
- Signs of sepsis (pale gums, hypothermia, tachycardia) with oral lesions — secondary bacteremia
- Rapid weight loss > 2 weeks — aggressive nutritional support needed
- Neurological signs with fever — uncommon but serious
