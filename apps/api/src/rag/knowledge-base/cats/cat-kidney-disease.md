## Disease Overview

Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD) is the most common chronic disease in older cats, affecting approximately 30–40% of cats over 10 years of age. Like dogs, cats lose nephrons silently and clinical signs emerge only when > 66–75% of function is lost. **SDMA (symmetric dimethylarginine)** is an important early biomarker in cats and can detect CKD before creatinine crosses reference ranges. Cats are also prone to concurrent hypertension and proteinuria, which must be managed to slow progression.

## Common Symptoms

- Polyuria / polydipsia (PU/PD)
- Weight loss, muscle wasting (very common in cats)
- Decreased appetite or anorexia
- Vomiting (uremic gastritis)
- Lethargy
- Unkempt haircoat
- Pale mucous membranes (anemia)
- Uremic breath
- Oral ulcers (advanced disease)

## CBC / Chemistry Patterns

- **Elevated CREA and BUN**: Core diagnostic finding; creatinine reference range in cats is lower than dogs — values > 1.6 mg/dL are concerning
- **Elevated SDMA**: Can indicate CKD at IRIS Stage 1 before creatinine is abnormal; valuable for early detection
- **Elevated PHOS**: Hyperphosphatemia; negative prognostic indicator and accelerates renal progression
- **Non-regenerative anemia** (low HCT, HGB, RBC): Extremely common in feline CKD; caused by reduced erythropoietin production
- **Hypoalbuminemia** (ALB): In protein-losing nephropathy or advanced disease

## IRIS Staging Reference (Creatinine-based — Cat)

| Stage | Creatinine (cat) | Notes                              |
| ----- | ---------------- | ---------------------------------- |
| 1     | < 1.6 mg/dL      | Non-azotemic; SDMA may be elevated |
| 2     | 1.6–2.8 mg/dL    | Mild azotemia                      |
| 3     | 2.9–5.0 mg/dL    | Moderate azotemia                  |
| 4     | > 5.0 mg/dL      | Severe azotemia; uremic signs      |

_Note: Creatinine thresholds differ between cats and dogs — use species-appropriate reference ranges._

## Interpretation Hints

- Cats have lower muscle mass than dogs; creatinine reference ranges are lower — a value of 2.0 mg/dL is more concerning in a cat than in a dog
- SDMA is especially valuable in cats because body condition and muscle loss affect creatinine — SDMA is not affected by muscle mass
- Anemia in feline CKD is typically non-regenerative (low reticulocytes) — helps differentiate from hemolytic or blood-loss anemia
- Concurrent M. haemofelis can worsen anemia — test for hemoplasmas in all anemic CKD cats
- Blood pressure measurement is essential: hypertension present in ~20–65% of CKD cats; causes retinal detachment and CNS signs

## Suggested Next Tests

- **Urinalysis with USG** — isosthenuria (USG 1.008–1.012) confirms inadequate concentration; essential for staging
- **Urine protein:creatinine ratio (UPC)** — quantifies proteinuria; sub-stages CKD (proteinuric vs. non-proteinuric)
- **Blood pressure measurement** — hypertension must be detected and treated
- **SDMA** — early CKD detection; especially useful in cats with low muscle mass
- **Phosphorus** — if not on panel; critical for management
- **Reticulocyte count** — confirms non-regenerative anemia
- **M. haemofelis PCR** — in all anemic CKD cats to rule out concurrent hemoplasma
- **Renal ultrasound** — assesses kidney size and architecture
- **Urine culture** — rules out pyelonephritis (can accelerate CKD progression)
