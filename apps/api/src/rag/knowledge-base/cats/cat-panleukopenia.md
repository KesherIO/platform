## Disease Overview

Feline Panleukopenia Virus (FPV), also called feline parvovirus or feline distemper, is a highly contagious parvovirus causing severe leukopenia, gastrointestinal destruction, and high mortality in unvaccinated cats — especially kittens. FPV is closely related to Canine Parvovirus CPV-2 and shares the same hallmark: **severe panleukopenia**. The virus is environmentally stable and persists for months. In utero infection can cause cerebellar hypoplasia in kittens.

## Common Symptoms

- Sudden onset depression, lethargy
- Severe vomiting and profuse diarrhea (may be hemorrhagic)
- High fever (hypothermia in terminal shock)
- Anorexia, dehydration
- Abdominal pain
- Collapse, signs of septic shock
- In utero: ataxia, intention tremor (cerebellar hypoplasia) in surviving kittens

## CBC / Chemistry Patterns

- **Panleukopenia** (WBC, NEU, LYM): Defining finding — total WBC often < 2,000/μL; sometimes < 500/μL
- **Thrombocytopenia** (PLT): Bone marrow depletion
- **Anemia** (HCT, HGB): From hemorrhagic diarrhea and bone marrow suppression
- **Hypoalbuminemia** (ALB): Protein-losing enteropathy
- **Prerenal azotemia** (BUN, CREA): Dehydration
- **Hypoglycemia** (GLU): Especially in kittens — requires active monitoring and correction

## Interpretation Hints

- Panleukopenia in a kitten or unvaccinated cat = FPV until proven otherwise
- Identical CBC pattern to Canine Parvovirus in dogs — severity correlates with neutrophil nadir
- Neutrophil count is the best prognostic indicator: < 500/μL carries grave prognosis; recovery trend = favorable
- Always check blood glucose in kittens — hypoglycemia is rapidly fatal
- Recently vaccinated cats can shed modified live vaccine virus and produce mild fecal antigen test positivity
- Differentiate from FeLV-induced pancytopenia using FeLV testing (FeLV causes more gradual onset)

## Suggested Next Tests

- **Fecal Parvovirus antigen SNAP test** — highly sensitive; cross-reacts with FPV and CPV-2; first-line test
- **FPV PCR (fecal or blood)** — confirms active infection; differentiates vaccine strain from wild-type
- **Serial CBC every 24–48 h** — WBC trend guides prognosis and hospitalization duration
- **Serial blood glucose monitoring** (q4–6h in kittens) — mandatory in young animals
- **Serum albumin** — guides fluid and colloid therapy decisions
- **FeLV/FIV testing** — rule out concurrent retroviral immunosuppression
- **Blood culture** — if deterioration despite appropriate supportive care (suspect bacterial translocation)
