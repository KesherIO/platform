## Disease Overview

Liver disease in cats encompasses hepatic lipidosis (the most common feline hepatopathy), inflammatory cholangitis/cholangiohepatitis complex, lymphoma, and other conditions. **Hepatic lipidosis (HL)** is unique to cats and occurs when prolonged anorexia (even 2–7 days) triggers excessive fat mobilization to the liver, overwhelming hepatic metabolism. It is a serious, potentially life-threatening condition that is also reversible with aggressive nutritional support. **Icterus (jaundice) is a key feature** and in cats is most commonly hepatic or post-hepatic in origin.

## Common Symptoms

- Anorexia (often the inciting cause in hepatic lipidosis)
- Marked weight loss
- Icterus (yellow sclera, skin, mucous membranes) — very common in feline liver disease
- Vomiting
- Lethargy, depression
- Drooling, ptyalism
- Hepatomegaly (palpable or on imaging)
- Neurological signs (hepatic encephalopathy) in severe cases

## CBC / Chemistry Patterns

- **Elevated ALT and AST**: Hepatocellular injury; marked elevation common in hepatic lipidosis
- **Elevated ALP**: Important — in cats, ALP has a half-life of ~6 hours (vs. days in dogs), so even mild ALP elevation is clinically significant; not induced by steroids in cats
- **Elevated GGT**: Cholestasis; elevated GGT with relatively normal ALP in hepatic lipidosis can occur (lipidosis spares bile ducts more than inflammatory disease)
- **Elevated TBIL**: Often markedly elevated in hepatic lipidosis and cholangiohepatitis; visible as icterus
- **Hypoalbuminemia** (ALB): Chronic or severe hepatic synthetic failure
- **Low BUN**: Reduced hepatic urea cycle function — suggests hepatic synthetic failure
- **Low GLU**: Possible with severe hepatic failure; less common than in dogs

## Interpretation Hints

- In cats, **any ALP elevation is significant** — unlike dogs where ALP is commonly induced by steroids
- GGT elevation out of proportion to ALP can suggest hepatic lipidosis (ALP spared while GGT rises)
- Icterus in cats is most commonly hepatic or post-hepatic — hemolytic icterus (e.g., M. haemofelis) is less common but must be differentiated
- Anorexia history of ≥ 2 days in an overweight cat = hepatic lipidosis must be suspected
- Hepatic lipidosis is often secondary — identify and treat the underlying cause (e.g., pancreatitis, IBD, dental disease causing anorexia)
- Low BUN with low ALB and icterus = strong indicator of hepatic synthetic failure — serious prognostic sign

## Suggested Next Tests

- **Abdominal ultrasound** — liver echogenicity (diffuse hyperechogenicity in lipidosis); biliary tree evaluation
- **Liver fine needle aspirate (FNA) + cytology** — vacuolated hepatocytes with lipid confirm hepatic lipidosis; minimally invasive
- **Pre- and post-prandial bile acids** — sensitive hepatic function test; useful when TBIL is normal
- **Coagulation profile (PT, aPTT)** — liver produces coagulation factors; assess before FNA or biopsy
- **Pancreatitis panel (feline PLI)** — concurrent pancreatitis common in cats with liver disease
- **FeLV/FIV testing** — retroviral disease can cause lymphoma (hepatic)
- **Urinalysis** — bilirubin, casts
- **Liver biopsy** — definitive diagnosis; requires coagulation assessment first
