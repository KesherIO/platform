## Disease Overview

Feline Immunodeficiency Virus (FIV) is a lentivirus (retrovirus) that progressively destroys CD4+ T-lymphocytes, leading to immunodeficiency. Transmission occurs primarily through bite wounds (high virus concentration in saliva). Outdoor male cats are at highest risk. FIV progresses through acute, asymptomatic carrier, and AIDS-like terminal phases over months to years. Most FIV-positive cats have a prolonged asymptomatic period and can live relatively normal lives until terminal immunosuppression.

## Common Symptoms

**Acute phase (weeks after infection):**

- Fever, lymphadenopathy
- Mild leukopenia

**Asymptomatic carrier phase (months to years):**

- No clinical signs; positive serology

**Progressive/terminal phase:**

- Recurrent infections (oral, respiratory, skin, urinary)
- Chronic stomatitis/gingivitis (very common)
- Weight loss, poor body condition
- Anemia, leukopenia
- Neurological signs (FIV is neurotropic)
- Ocular disease

## CBC / Chemistry Patterns

- **Lymphopenia** (LYM): Key and progressive finding; CD4:CD8 ratio inversion in advanced disease
- **Neutropenia** (NEU): Advanced disease; predisposes to opportunistic infections
- **Non-regenerative anemia** (HCT, HGB, RBC): Anemia of chronic disease; may worsen with co-infections
- **Hyperglobulinemia** (GLOB): Chronic immune stimulation; common in FIV-positive cats
- **Hypoalbuminemia** (ALB): In cachectic or terminally ill animals

## Interpretation Hints

- FIV-positive cats in the asymptomatic phase may have a completely normal CBC and chemistry
- Lymphopenia in any adult cat with chronic recurrent infections warrants FIV testing
- Chronic stomatitis/gingivitis that is refractory to treatment is strongly associated with FIV
- FIV co-infection worsens the prognosis for M. haemofelis, FeLV, and other concurrent diseases
- Standard FIV antibody test detects exposure, not active infection; vaccinated cats will test positive — use PCR to differentiate
- Neonates born to FIV+ queens may have passively acquired maternal antibodies — retest at 6 months

## Suggested Next Tests

- **FIV antibody SNAP test or ELISA** — standard in-clinic test; detects antibodies
- **FIV PCR (blood)** — confirms active infection; differentiates vaccinated cats from truly infected ones
- **FeLV antigen test** — always test concurrently (FIV/FeLV dual test)
- **CBC with differential** — lymphocyte count and neutrophil status guide clinical management
- **CD4/CD8 ratio** — available at reference labs; helps stage immunosuppression
- **Oral/dental exam + culture** — if stomatitis present
- **Urine culture** — if recurrent urinary tract infections
