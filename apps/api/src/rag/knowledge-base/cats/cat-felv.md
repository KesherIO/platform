## Disease Overview

Feline Leukemia Virus (FeLV) is an oncogenic retrovirus that infects cats through prolonged close contact (mutual grooming, shared food bowls, bite wounds). It is the leading cause of **non-regenerative anemia** and virus-associated neoplasia (lymphoma, leukemia) in cats. FeLV can lead to several outcomes: regressive infection (cleared or latent), progressive infection (persistent viremia with organ damage), or focal infection. Progressively infected cats have a significantly shortened lifespan.

## Common Symptoms

- Pale mucous membranes, weakness (anemia)
- Lethargy, anorexia, weight loss
- Lymphadenopathy
- Recurrent infections (immunosuppression)
- Lymphoma signs: masses, dyspnea, pleural effusion
- Neurological signs
- Reproductive failure, abortion

## CBC / Chemistry Patterns

- **Non-regenerative anemia** (low HCT, HGB, RBC): Most common hematologic abnormality; bone marrow suppression
- **Macrocytosis** (elevated MCV): FeLV-associated macrocytosis without anemia can be an early clue
- **Thrombocytopenia** (PLT): Bone marrow suppression; also thrombocytopenic purpura in some cases
- **Leukopenia or leukocytosis**: Leukopenia from marrow failure; marked leukocytosis from leukemic transformation
- **Pancytopenia**: In severe myelosuppression — grave prognosis

## Interpretation Hints

- Non-regenerative anemia in a cat = FeLV test mandatory; it is the most common cause
- Macrocytosis (high MCV) without anemia in a cat is a red flag for FeLV — often an early finding
- FeLV-associated lymphoma is the most common feline neoplasm — suspect in cats with mediastinal mass or GI masses
- Unlike FIV, FeLV is a direct bone marrow suppressor — pancytopenia indicates marrow infiltration
- FeLV and FIV co-infection worsens prognosis for both viruses
- Regressive (latent) FeLV may test antigen-negative but PCR-positive

## Suggested Next Tests

- **FeLV antigen SNAP test or ELISA** — detects p27 antigen (viremia); first-line in-clinic test
- **Confirmatory IFA (indirect fluorescent antibody)** — confirms progressive viremia in ELISA-positive cats
- **FeLV PCR (blood or bone marrow)** — distinguishes regressive from progressive infection
- **FIV antibody test** — always test concurrently
- **Bone marrow aspirate** — if pancytopenia or suspected marrow infiltration/neoplasia
- **Chest radiographs + abdominal ultrasound** — evaluate for lymphoma, pleural effusion, organomegaly
- **Reticulocyte count** — confirms non-regenerative pattern (absent reticulocytosis)
