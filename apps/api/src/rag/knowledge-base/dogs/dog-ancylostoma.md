## Disease Overview

Hookworm disease is caused by _Ancylostoma caninum_ (most pathogenic) and related species. These blood-feeding nematodes attach to the intestinal mucosa and cause significant blood loss. Infection occurs via skin penetration, ingestion of larvae, or transmammary transmission. Young puppies are at highest risk for life-threatening anemia. Chronic infections in adult dogs cause protein-losing enteropathy and iron-deficiency anemia.

## Common Symptoms

- Pale mucous membranes (anemia)
- Weakness, lethargy
- Melena (dark, tarry stools) or frank hematochezia
- Weight loss, poor body condition
- Diarrhea (may be bloody)
- Skin lesions between toes (cutaneous larval migration)
- Sudden death in heavily infected neonates

## CBC / Chemistry Patterns

- **Microcytic, hypochromic anemia** (low HCT, HGB, RBC, MCV, MCH): Classic pattern with chronic blood loss and iron depletion
- **Regenerative anemia** expected in acute heavy infection (reticulocytosis)
- **Eosinophilia** (EOS): Common with larval migration; not always present in chronic adult infections
- **Hypoalbuminemia** (low ALB): From enteric protein loss and reduced intake — may be severe
- **Low TP**: Panhypoproteinemia in severe or chronic cases

## Interpretation Hints

- Microcytic hypochromic anemia in a puppy or young dog = hookworms until proven otherwise
- Eosinophilia + anemia in a young dog with outdoor exposure should always prompt fecal evaluation
- Hypoalbuminemia without liver enzyme elevation or proteinuria = protein-losing enteropathy → check for parasites
- Adult dogs may have low-grade chronic infections with mild lab changes and intermittent soft stools
- Peracute anemia in neonates can be fatal before eggs are detectable in feces — treat empirically

## Suggested Next Tests

- **Fecal flotation (zinc sulfate or sodium nitrate)** — detects eggs; false-negative possible in peracute neonatal cases
- **Fecal PCR** — confirms species and mixed infections
- **Iron panel (serum iron, TIBC, ferritin)** — confirms iron-deficiency pattern if available
- **Serum albumin and total protein** — quantifies enteric protein loss
- **Fecal occult blood test** — if melena not grossly visible
- **Bone marrow evaluation** — only if anemia unexplained after parasitic workup
