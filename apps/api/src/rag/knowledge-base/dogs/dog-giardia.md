## Disease Overview

Giardiasis is an intestinal infection caused by the flagellate protozoan _Giardia duodenalis_ (also called _G. intestinalis_ or _G. lamblia_). It is one of the most common intestinal parasites in dogs worldwide. Infection occurs via ingestion of cysts from contaminated water, soil, or feces. Many infections are subclinical, especially in adult dogs. Young, immunocompromised, or shelter dogs are most severely affected.

## Common Symptoms

- Soft, pale, greasy, malodorous diarrhea (often intermittent)
- Weight loss in chronic cases
- Flatulence, borborygmi
- Generally minimal systemic signs
- Dehydration in severe or prolonged cases

## CBC / Chemistry Patterns

- **CBC is usually within normal limits** in uncomplicated Giardia
- **Mild eosinophilia** (EOS): Possible but inconsistent — more common with helminth parasites
- **Low ALB and TP** (hypoproteinemia): May occur with chronic, severe malabsorption — not typical in mild/moderate infection
- Chemistry panel is generally unremarkable unless secondary complications exist

## Interpretation Hints

- Normal CBC and chemistry do NOT rule out Giardia — standard blood work is not sensitive for this diagnosis
- Giardia should be suspected whenever there is chronic intermittent soft/pale diarrhea, especially in young or shelter dogs
- Low albumin with normal liver enzymes + diarrhea history → consider protein-losing enteropathy; Giardia is one cause
- Giardia can co-exist with other intestinal parasites; comprehensive fecal evaluation is recommended
- Zoonotic potential: _G. duodenalis_ can infect humans — relevant for household counseling

## Suggested Next Tests

- **Fecal antigen test (ELISA or SNAP)** — most sensitive in-clinic test; preferred over flotation alone
- **Zinc sulfate fecal flotation** — detects cysts; sensitivity improved with centrifugation
- **Direct saline smear** — may detect motile trophozoites in fresh diarrhea samples
- **Giardia PCR (fecal)** — high sensitivity and specificity; available at reference labs
- **Serum albumin and total protein** — if diarrhea is chronic; assess for protein-losing enteropathy
- **Rule out other intestinal parasites** (Ancylostoma, Isospora, Cryptosporidium) concurrently
